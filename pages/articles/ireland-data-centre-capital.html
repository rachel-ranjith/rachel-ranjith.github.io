Ireland's Hidden Cost of Being Europe's Data Centre Capital

November 2025 · 6 min read · Read on Trinity News ↗

Ireland's data centres now consume more electricity than urban dwellings, raising questions about the country's legally binding climate commitments.

Ireland has become Europe's data centre capital, hosting approximately 89 operational facilities that now consume a staggering 22% of the country's total metered electricity. This figure surpasses consumption by both rural homes (10%) and urban homes (18%) marking a dramatic shift from just 5% in 2015. According to EirGrid, this figure could also reach as high as 31% by 2027.

Ireland is currently falling short of its legally binding climate targets, which requires a 51% reduction in greenhouse gas emissions by 2030 compared to 2018 levels. Even under optimistic projections, Ireland is expected to miss its 2030 goal by at least 10%. The rapid growth of electricity-hungry data centres directly threatens these goals at a time when the country is attempting to transition away from fossil fuels.

The situation in Ireland mirrors current developments in the United States, where the AI boom is driving unprecedented energy demand. According to BloombergNEF, data centres will account for 8.6% of all US electricity demand by 2035, double their current 3.5% share. Despite corporate pledges of sustainability, the International Energy Agency reports remind us that the gap between rhetoric and reality is stark.

40% of data centre electricity demand is met by natural gas, nuclear and coal supply over 20% and 15% respectively, and renewables supplying just under 25%.

Major tech companies have made public commitments to renewable energy. Google pledges carbon-free power by 2030, Microsoft aims to be carbon negative by the same year, and Amazon Web Services describes itself as the world's largest corporate buyer of clean energy. However, these commitments heavily rely on renewable energy certificates, which allow companies to claim their electricity came from renewable sources even when the actual power they consume comes from the grid's general mix.

While some renewable energy certificates help fund new projects, many are "bundled" certificates that provide side revenue for existing facilities. Critics argue the prices are often too low to justify new investment decisions. The Biden administration attempted to address this in January 2025 by mandating that developers building on government lands bring online enough clean energy to match their power needs. Donald Trump revoked that executive order within months, calling renewable energy a "scam" and cutting tax credits for wind and solar in his spending bill.

Ireland actively courted data centre investment through supportive economic policies, including tax incentives and streamlined planning permissions. The country's appeal includes its English-speaking workforce, EU access, cool climate for lower cooling costs, and common law legal system. Dublin now hosts the largest data centre cluster in Europe, surpassing Frankfurt, London, Amsterdam, and Paris. Major facilities from Google, Meta, Amazon, and Microsoft are clustered in industrial parks in Lucan, Clonshaugh, and Blanchardstown, with Meta's Meath campus consuming electricity equivalent to a city like Kilkenny.

Ireland already faces the highest proportion of electricity consumption from data centres in the EU.

At 18% of national demand, this is more than triple the Netherlands at 5.2% and nearly four times Luxembourg at 4.8%. The Commission for Regulation of Utilities warned in 2021 of "significant risk to electricity security supply," directly attributing this to data centre growth. EirGrid imposed a moratorium on new Dublin data centres until 2028 due to grid capacity constraints rather than environmental concerns.

Ireland also relies more heavily on fossil fuels for electricity than its European neighbours. Dr Paul Deane of University College Cork noted that "building a data centre in any other country in northwest Europe would produce less climate pollution than building it in Ireland." The country's renewable energy infrastructure lags behind competitors, meaning each new data centre adds disproportionate carbon emissions.

Data centres require constant uptime and operate fossil fuel backup generators for when the grid fails. Since 2005, Irish data centres with Greenhouse Gas Emissions permits have released 181,553 tonnes of CO2, with the vast majority emitted since 2017. EdgeConneX's Dublin campus alone has emitted nearly 130,000 tonnes since 2017, equivalent to running over 3,400 cars annually. These emissions occur in addition to grid consumption.

A 2018 IDA-commissioned study claimed data centres contributed €7.13 billion to the Irish economy since 2010, creating employment for technicians, engineers, and managers. Partnerships with educational institutions, including IT Sligo's degree program with Google, align with government STEM initiatives.

However, data centre approval currently relies on case-by-case assessments of grid connection capacity, with no comprehensive national policy framework. Policy decisions fall under the responsibility of energy utilities granting connection rights. EirGrid has warned that large data centres may leave Ireland if connection agreements cannot be signed due to grid constraints.

With over 40 data centres in planning stages or granted permission, the industry's growth shows no signs of slowing.

Adding more facilities is likely to offset gains from renewable energy investment, making the 2030 climate targets increasingly difficult to achieve. Data centres also consume large quantities of water for cooling operations, adding pressure to Ireland's already strained water management systems.

US utility companies are investing record sums to meet data centre demand, with capital expenditure expected to reach $212.1 billion in 2025, a 129% increase from a decade ago. American residential electricity prices are projected to increase between 15% and 40% by 2030, according to ICF consulting group. Ireland faces similar pressures as utilities invest in infrastructure to support data centre growth, with costs potentially flowing through to household bills.

The question facing policymakers is whether Ireland can accommodate continued data centre expansion while meeting climate commitments and maintaining grid stability. With no clear national strategy and EirGrid's warnings about capacity constraints, the country appears caught between short-term economic benefits and long-term environmental obligations.
